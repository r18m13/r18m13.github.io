Robotics Project

Autonomous Wall-Climbing Quadruped Robot with Obstacle Avoidance

A 3D-printed, Arduino-controlled quadruped robot extended with magnetic adhesion and sensing capabilities to enable autonomous wall climbing and collision-free navigation.

Role

Academic Project

Timeline

2022

Technologies

Arduino UNO SOLIDWORKS 3D Printing Embedded Systems Robotics Sensors Kinematics

Overview

This project combines a 3D-printed omni-directional quadruped robot with an autonomous wall-climbing system. The quadruped platform was initially developed for stable ground locomotion using servo-actuated limbs and Arduino-based control.

Building upon this base, the robot was extended with magnetic adhesion mechanisms and obstacle detection sensors to enable vertical mobility. The resulting system demonstrates a progression from ground-based locomotion to autonomous wall climbing with collision avoidance, highlighting modular mechanical design and adaptable control strategies.

Key Features

3D-Printed Quadruped Base

Designed modular quadruped limbs in SOLIDWORKS and fabricated the robot using 3D-printed components to achieve omni-directional ground mobility.

Arduino-Based Locomotion Control

Implemented gait control and kinematic validation on Arduino UNO to test coordinated servo actuation and movement strategies.

Magnetic Wall-Climbing Extension

Integrated magnetic adhesion mechanisms into the quadruped design, enabling stable vertical movement on metallic surfaces.

Obstacle Detection and Avoidance

Added proximity and obstacle detection sensors to support autonomous navigation and collision avoidance during wall-climbing operation.

Vertical Locomotion Adaptation

Extended omni-directional quadruped locomotion principles to support controlled transitions between horizontal and vertical surfaces.

Technical Implementation

The quadruped robot was designed in SOLIDWORKS with a focus on modularity, allowing the same mechanical platform to support both ground locomotion and wall climbing. Servo motors provided joint actuation, while Arduino UNO handled real-time control and gait sequencing.

For wall climbing, magnetic adhesion modules were incorporated into the limb design. Obstacle detection sensors supplied distance feedback, enabling basic autonomous decision-making for navigation and collision avoidance. Control logic was adapted to maintain stability and adhesion during vertical motion.

Results & Impact

The project successfully demonstrated a single robotic platform evolving from a ground-based quadruped into an autonomous wall-climbing robot. The system validated mechanical reuse, scalable control design, and the feasibility of extending quadruped locomotion to vertical environments.

This work highlights the effectiveness of modular robotic design and serves as a foundation for future research in inspection robots, search-and-rescue platforms, and adaptive multi-terrain locomotion systems.
